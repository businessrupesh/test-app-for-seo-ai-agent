<h3>What is the difference between over-ear and on-ear?</h3>
<p>Over-ear headphones cup your entire ear and create a seal around it. On-ear headphones rest directly on the ear. Over-ear is generally more comfortable for long sessions; on-ear is lighter and more portable.</p>

<h3>How important is noise cancellation?</h3>
<p>If you commute on planes or work in a noisy office, noise cancellation is worth paying for. If you mostly listen in quiet rooms, it is a nice extra but not essential.</p>

<h3>Are wireless headphones worth it?</h3>
<p>For most people, yes. The freedom of not being tethered to your device outweighs the small audio quality penalty most listeners cannot hear.</p>

<h3>Should I get open-back or closed-back?</h3>
<p>Closed-back is what you want unless you have a dedicated quiet listening room. Open-back leaks sound both ways and is mostly for at-home listening.</p>

<h3>How much should I spend?</h3>
<p>You can get genuinely great headphones for around 200 dollars. Above that you pay diminishing returns for marginal improvements. Below 100 dollars you can still find solid options if you read reviews.</p>

<h2>Battery life</h2>
<p>Battery life matters more than people expect. Look for at least 20 hours on wireless. Most reasonable headphones today claim 30-40 hours, which is enough that you only charge them once a week.</p>
